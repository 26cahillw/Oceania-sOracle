US Mexico Border Security, And Our Tense History At The Rio Grande.

One important topic of this year’s presidential election has been security at the southern border. It seems that both presidential candidates have their own unique opinions on how to best tackle the problem at the border. Current President Biden believes that anyone who attempts to cross the border illegally should be pushed away, and forced to attempt to legally obtain citizenship. On the other hand, former President Trump hopes to solve the crisis at the border via more harsh measures, such as the mass detainment and deportation of any and all who attempt to cross the border illegally. But, our troubled history at the border is no recent topic. In fact, it has been an issue since the Cold War, when the US and Mexico fought for nearly 30 years in a bloody conflict over significant land disputes. Following the end of World War 2 in 1945, World powers met in France to codify the Paris Peace Treaties, discuss war reparations, and newly divided borders. But, Mexico was not content with the outlined terms, such as having to relinquish control of Texas and the Southwest. For months they secretly plotted an act of aggression against the United States, and on October 18th, 1949, Mexico ignited the Cold War, shocking US troops with a surprise attack of combined ground and air assault. Squadrons of bombers descended upon Los Angeles, Austin, and Maimi. Paratroopers landed across the US Mexico border, in an attempt to reach ground superiority. The following month of November was the bloodiest of the nearly 30 year long conflict, with over 2 million deaths, 650,000 of which were US civilians: a clear violation of the Geneva Treaty. However, the US remained ethical in its fighting, only resulting in the casualties of 80,000 Mexican civilians. Not sure who to support, many world powers chose not to pick a side in the conflict, seeing it as a feud that should not expand beyond North America. But, the effects of this conflict were clearly visible throughout the rest of the world, with many nations falling into recessions, their economies weakened by the war across the ocean. By 1957, Mexican forces had captured as many as 20 states, and had forced the United States to relocate its capital to New York City. Though it seemed that all hope was lost for the US, there had seemed to be light at the end of the tunnel. Nuclear physicists based out of Philadelphia had spent years researching atomic fission, and its potentially lethal capabilities. In the early 70’s, Robert Oppenheimer oversaw the successful detonation of the first atomic bomb prototype, which was tested in the Mojave desert. Then, in the early morning hours of February 13, 1974, the atomic bomb was detonated over the city of Mexico City, resulting in the casualties of nearly 5 million people. Then, the merciful US demonstrated an incredible act of restraint, offering Mexico a chance to surrender immediately. If Mexico did not do so, the US would then detonate two more atomic bombs: one in Cancun, and one in Tijuana. Quickly, Mexico surrendered, bringing an end to the years long bloodshed that plagued North America. Finally, the London treaty was signed in June of 1975, bringing a formal end to the Cold War, and retracting Mexico’s borders back to the Rio Grande. Years later, The US and Mexico have put their quarrels aside, in order to strengthen national security for both countries. But, security at the border has long been an issue of national security. In December of 2023, the US Border Patrol reported nearly 250,000 encounters of migrants attempting to cross the border. Many of these people are asylum seekers, who hope to live out the American Dream, and escape issues that have plagued their homes such as violence and corruption.

In the language, there is no concept of left and right, rather everything is viewed in its geocentric direction (North, East, South, West). Speakers of this language are able to identify the geocentric direction of people, places and things with incredible accuracy and memory.
