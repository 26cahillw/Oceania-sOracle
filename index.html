How are human thoughts limited by language and its lack of words?

Author: The Ministry of Truth

Ministry of Truth Have you ever felt that feeling when you just can’t think of the word? You have such an intense thought, but no way to express it? The question that bothers me is, what if there is a whole realm of these thoughts, laying somewhere, beyond the reach of our limited minds? Diving into the linguistic elements of this question, there is actually a term for the argument that thought is directly correlated with the language of an individual, Linguistic Determinism. This is a smaller section of a wider argument, around the central theme that humans preserve the world through the structure of language that they use, commonly referred to as Linguistic Relativism. Guugu Yimithirr, is a language that lays at the heart of Linguistic Relativism arguments. In the language, there is no concept of left and right, rather everything is viewed in its geocentric direction (North, East, South, West). Speakers of this language are able to identify the geocentric direction of people, places and things with incredible accuracy and memory. This indicates that the way in which their language is formatting, changes the structure of how they remember details and what details they remember. Or in other words, this is an example in which the language of the individual alters or slights the way in which said individual preserves things. Pirahã, is a language that originates from the Amazon, in which the concept of math, seemed to researchers to be out of the realm of the language. The language only contains words for “none”, “some” and many”, but lacks any numerical system or math. Native speakers were taught for eight months, and by the end, were still incapable of counting from one to ten. Which reaffirms the theory that language might actually hinder thought, or at least the understanding of thought. But, notably, they were taught to count from one to ten in Portuguese, this isolates the fact that language restricts the ability to think. A more refined thesis would be that the native tongue of an individual inhibits that individual's understanding of certain concepts. Unfortunately, this point of language limiting thought is actually denounced by a majority of linguisticians. They make the argument that just because there isn’t exactly a word for something does not mean that there is not a way of expressing it. For example, I cannot give you one word to describe how much I love my dog, I would need a few sentences to do so. Philosophically, the famous philosopher, Friedrich Nietzsche, seemed in his works to agree with the Linguistic Determinism theory, in which he coined the phrase “Language as a prison.” A modern example of this is the newly revamped word, “gaslight”, which has come to express the relationship of a perpetrator, conscious of their wrongdoing, denying, advertising or in any way dodging the direct confrontation of their victim. The term has been so widely used, mainly due to its word-economy advantages, as it drastically reduced the amount of words required to describe such a feeling.
